I'm a creative developer with a background in news. I changed direction in my career because I got jealous of all the fun developers were having building the final package.

My skills now include Ruby, Rails, JavaScript, jQuery, HTML/CSS, Backbone and PostgreSQL.

I believe strongly that any creative project should be driven by concern for clarity and substance, and I aspire to apply this to my code as much as my written work.

Until recently I worked as a librarian at NPR. Together with editorial staff, I gathered the various facts, data and multimedia tidbits that go into crafting a rich story experience.

I contributed to a variety of editorial projects, including things like @todayin1963 and Pop-Up Politics, and a few original works of my own.

I love the storytelling potential of structured data. Because I'm a DIY person by nature, I set out to learn to code. I've just finished a 12-week intensive course with General Assembly to learn full-stack web development.

Now I am looking for the perfect opportunity to put these skills to use, coding rich experiences that engage audiences through a sense of personal relevance and delight.

"Who Said That?" is a trivia game where players guess which member of Congress issued a Tweet drawn at random from the Twitter API. It is the first project I completed at GA WDI (General Assembly's Web Development Immersive).

I built this app with Ruby on Rails in a 5 day sprint. It features a hand-made user authentication (a requirement for the assignment), and tracks player scores on a leaderboard.

"Partify" allows you to crowd-source the playlist for your next gathering, drawing from the Spotify and YouTube APIs. It was developed as a 5 day group project during WDI, and is also built on Rails.

I liked this project because it solves a real-world problem, and I got to focus on designing and building the front end. Future plans include improving the mobile experience, especially on the homepage. We agreed this would be a great candidate for Backbone, to give the code better structure and have smooth one page functionality.

"PT Helper" is designed to help physical therapy patients keep track of their assigned exercises and progress during therapy.

I built this Backbone app with from-scratch user authentication during a 5 day sprint. In the next cycle of development, I plan to create a timer function that will lead patients through their PT routine and record completed exercises in a database. I also plan to add photo-upload functionality, so that patients can add images or video of their therapist demonstrating exercises.

User stories for this project are on Pivotal Tracker.
